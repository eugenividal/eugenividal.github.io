Inequalities in cycling participation and policies for its diversification

In the late 1990s and early 2000s, the field of "inequalities in transport" emerged and researchers and policy-makers began thinking of policies to increase access and opportunities for transport disadvantaged populations. These policies were based basically on improvements in public transport supply. However, less attention was paid to the role that access to active transport in general, and cycling in particular, could play in reducing social inequalities related to transport. It is not until the recent resurgence of cycling (in some cities) and the boom of bicycle sharing systems, in the last decade, that the bicycle has started to be seen as a powerful tool for social equity and a significant interest about cycling equity has grown.

Access to cycling can help not only to increase social inclusion among disadvantaged populations but also to reduce health inequalities. Deprived populations suffer from higher levels of obesity and poorer health condition than affluent populations, and this is largely due to their lower physical activity. Travelling by bicycle (or on foot) could, therefore, be of great help to address this problem. In addition, cycling may also build social cohesion, particularly among marginalised young, through the creation of cycling movements or spaces related to the bicycle.

The project seeks to answer the following research questions: • Why cycling is relevant for disadvantaged populations? • What is their current level of participation? • Which are their main constraints? • Are cycling infrastructure equally distributed among socio-spatial areas? • How can cycling policies be designed to ensure they tackle transport inequality?

Combining research methods, this project will give the answer to all these questions in order to better understand cycling among disadvantaged populations and provide effective policies to tackle the identified inequalities.

The supervisors of the project bring together expertise in geospatial analysis (Dr Robin Lovelace), in travel behaviour and active travel (Dr Eva Heinen; Professor Jillian Anable); and in statistical modelling and data analytics (Dr Richard Mann).
